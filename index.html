Show me the maths

Each year your super balance does two things: grows from investment returns, and increases from your boss's contribution. Then it does it all again next year. That's compound interest.

new balance = old balance × (1 + r) + 0.85 × SG × salary
new salary   = old salary × (1 + g)

What the letters mean:

  • r = investment return (we're using 7% — long-run average for a balanced super fund)
  • SG = super guarantee, which is 12% of your salary in 2026
  • g = how much your salary grows each year (we're using 3%)
  • 0.85 means 85% — the government takes 15% tax off super contributions on the way in

Worked example — first year on $48,000:

Boss puts in: 12% × $48,000 = $5,760
After 15% tax: 0.85 × $5,760 = $4,896 lands in super
Investment growth on $0 starting balance: $0
End of year 1 balance: $4,896
Year 2 starts with: $4,896 × 1.07 + new contribution…

The lesson: The formula doesn't change. But because each year builds on the last, the gap between starting at 18 vs starting at 25 is enormous — that's the power of time on compound interest.
